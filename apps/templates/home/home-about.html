Introduction

Medical clinicians are trained to identify and treat diseases. Physicians, in the spirit of the Hippocratic Oath, vow to use their ability and judgment to help the sick, regardless of whom the sick might be. Thus, it is often assumed that the professionals in the medical community hold unbiased attitudes toward their patients, including toward individuals with opioid use disorder (OUD).

It turns out that the opposite is true. Physicians often have worse attitudes toward individuals with substance use disorders (SUDs), more so than toward individuals with other medical and mental health diagnoses. Further, these attitudes may deteriorate over time. Those who have been practicing for longer have been shown to have even worse attitudes toward patients with SUDs, and the worst toward opioid use disorder.

The American Academy for Addiction Medicine defines addiction as a treatable, chronic medical disease involving complex interactions among brain circuits, genetics, the environment, and an individual’s life experiences.

People with addiction use substances or engage in behaviors that become compulsive and often continue despite harmful consequences. Prevention efforts and treatment approaches for addiction are generally as successful as those for other chronic diseases.

According to the World Health Organization, drug addiction is the number one most stigmatized social problem, even over poverty, incarceration, homelessness, mental illness, and alcoholism. Defined as an attribute, behavior, or condition that is socially discrediting, stigma stands as one of the largest barriers to individual recovery. One way to prevent such destructiveness is to identify and eradicate its foundations.

Stigma stands as a crucial determinant of health. Experiencing negative labels associated with use lowers self-esteem, which in turn lowers self-efficacy, which then might lead one to relapse or initiate the use of substances. Stigma also decreases social support. If one is viewed as someone who has a lesser social value, people are less likely to help. In this vein, stigma also limits one's access to treatment. Many psychosocial treatment modalities target the foundation on which stigma might be perpetuated, such as interpersonal isolation and social punishment due to use.

To explore this question, let's put substance use disorders in context. In 2020, 40.3 20.8 million Americans 12+ years old —approximately 6.5% of the population—met the criteria for a SUD in the past year. This was an increase of about 20 million from 2015. More specifically in 2020, 28.3 million people met the criteria for an alcohol use disorder, and 18.4 million people had been diagnosed with an illicit drug use disorder.

While these numbers are overwhelming, the numbers for overdose deaths are even more so. In the United States from 2001-2015, 530,000 people died from drug overdoses. Alarmingly, suicide tolls have been rising steadily as well. In short, SUDs impact many Americans, and nearly everyone knows someone who has been affected by the disorder. In addition, the magnitude of these disorders and death rates have resulted in significant impacts on society, including its economic well-being. Likewise, the strain on the healthcare system is felt even by those who do not have OUD.

Research has consistently found that clinician' negative attitudes toward patients with OUD result in worse quality of care. Negative attitudes have been shown to manifest in clinicians' beliefs that the patients with OUD are manipulative, lack motivation, and are violent. In addition, empathy is a key element of effective medical care, and negative clinical attitudes almost always result in reduced empathy for patients. In short, patient-centered care models are enfeebled, and actual treatment outcomes deteriorate.

During training, most health care professionals learn that addiction is an illness. Research into the neurobiology and the neurocircuitry of addiction is actively progressing, and the disease model of addiction is now robustly supported.

Despite these advances, many health care professionals still believe that addiction and substance use disorders are primarily moral failings. Clinicians may feel that if a person could just “get it together”, the problem would resolve. This mentality partly explains why attitudes toward patients with OUD are still negative in spite of enhanced training on how to care for these individuals.

A major 2011 study compared clinicians' attitudes toward patients with SUDs to their attitudes toward patients with depression or diabetes. The findings were clear: physicians, psychiatrists, psychologists, nurses, and social workers had less regard for individuals with SUDs than they did toward individuals who suffered from other illnesses, such as diabetes and depression.

The evidence suggests that these negative attitudes are not stagnant. While they form at the beginning of training, they appear to worsen over time. A study of psychiatry residents found that they stigmatized patients with SUDs more than patients with other mental health diagnoses—and these negative attitudes towards patients with SUDs were worse for more senior residents. Post-graduate year (PGY) 3 or 4 residents had significantly worse attitudes toward patients with SUDs than did junior residents (PGY 1 or 2).

Additional research has also found a decline in attitudes towards individuals with SUDs over time. A survey of medical students at Johns Hopkins Medical School compared the medical students' attitudes toward those with SUDs to the attitudes held by members of the senior house staff. In line with our findings, the senior house staff held more negative attitudes toward individuals with SUDs than did the medical students. In other words, the most knowledgeable, successful, and highly-trained physicians had worse attitudes than their students.

Likewise, research has found that feelings of satisfaction with working with individuals with SUDs decreased over years of training; at the same time, beliefs that individuals with SUDs were high utilizers of health care services increased.

Given the overwhelming evidence of attitudinal negativity, we might wonder whether any physicians hold positive attitudes toward individuals with SUDs. It turns out that one subset, in particular, has less stigma toward individuals with SUDs: clinicians who have chosen to work with such patients. While this alone is interesting, more research is needed to determine which attributes and cognitions might distinguish these clinicians from their colleagues.

Research has consistently found that clinicians' negative attitudes toward patients with SUDs result in worse quality of care. What contributes to such attitudes? One school of thought is that lack of education and knowledge on how to care for individuals with SUDs may lead to health care professionals' negative attitudes. As a result, medical schools and other training programs have increasingly added addiction education and skills to their curricula. These interventions, however, have not been sufficient to improve attitudes. That is, it appears that increasing knowledge through educational courses is helpful but not, in itself, a solution

Addiction is often considered an illness characterized by “loss of free will”. In other words, the neurobiological systems become rewired to depend on the substance, in such a way that guides behaviors and cognitions to maintain usage at all costs, and it becomes very difficult to modulate over time. The brain circuits affected by addiction include those involved with impulse executive functioning (i.e. decision-making), control, memory, reward, and stress. Over time, there are changes in brain functioning which is the cause of one's risk In using substances or continuously craving it during recovery. This knowledge can help counteract the belief that individuals with addiction are choosing to engage in destructive behaviors. Viewing the illness as a medical disease promotes greater compassion for our patients.

Clinicians tend to have few encounters with individuals with OUD who are in recovery. Unless they specialize in addiction, they encounter such patients when they are in acute use or withdrawal phases, and they often do not have sufficient time and resources to understand the person behind the disease. In short, they see only the difficult periods, and they do not see the successes. These negative encounters (and lack of positive encounters) lead to selfperpetuating negative beliefs about patients with OUD.

One variable mediating the degree of stigma toward an individual with an OUD is empathy, which might be driven if the clinician has increased personal experiences. Empathetic feelings can be achieved by taking the patient's perspective. In a clinical context, empathy refers to the physician's ability to understand a patient's emotional state, its surrounding circumstance, and then reflect such understanding back to the patient. Empathic physicians are able to communicate and listen with greater reflective tendency than those who aren't (24). Research has demonstrated that improved communication between physician and patient, of which empathy is a critical component, is associated with better adherence to medications, fewer physician mistakes, and greater patient satisfaction (9). Unfortunately, empathy has been found to decrease as one progresses through medical training (10; 11)

Clinicians tend to underestimate their own cognitive and affective states during these difficult patient presentations. Many clinicians identify with valorized medical ideals, such as saving lives and healing, which can be complicated by repeated difficult presentations of these patients. OUD, depending on its acuity, might challenge these ideals and a clinician's sense of feeling helpful to their patients.

For the previous reasons discussed, senior clinicians often are the most jaded. After a career of such difficulties, their opinions about individuals with OUD are not just negative, they are solidified. It is indubitable that these opinions and perspectives of senior clinicians impact younger clinicians' views. This is called the “hidden curriculum” of medical training: the unspoken attitudes and small details that supervising physicians convey when engaging with patients.
