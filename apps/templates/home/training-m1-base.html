Trauma-Informed Care

The majority of persons who inject opioids have a history of trauma either inflicted by others (e.g. physical, psychological, sexual, or emotional abuse, neglect, war, poverty, racism) or self-inflicted (i.e. physical trauma secondary to repeated drug use). The medical sequelae (e.g. skin abscess, endocarditis, cellulitis) and trauma (e.g. overdose, withdrawal) associated with injection drug use lead many PWID to seek care in hospital.

Trauma-informed care (TIC) is an approach that clinicians might use in order to respectfully meet these patients where they are regarding their comfort, trust, and openness toward their healthcare experience. One study demonstrated improvements in medical students’ perceived knowledge and comfort in treating sexual assault survivors following completion of a TIC module. Another study found patients reported a more trusting partnership with physicians who underwent TIC training compared to physicians who did not.

TIC involves five core principles: patient empowerment, choice, collaboration, safety, and trustworthiness.

Patient Empowerment

Using individuals' strengths to empower them in the development of their treatment.

Patient Choice

Informing patients regarding treatment options so they can choose the options they prefer.

Collaboration

Maximizing collaboration among health care staff, patients, and their families in organizational and treatment planning.

Safety

Developing health care settings and activities that ensure patients’ physical and emotional safety.

Trustworthiness

Creating clear expectations with patients about what proposed treatments entail, who will provide services, and how care will be provided.
